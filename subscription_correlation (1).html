INFORMATION GAIN ANALYSIS APPLIED TO SUBSCRIPTION IN TIELS HOP IOS¶

Introduction¶

Current situation:

The motivation for our study derives from the need of Product owners and Marketers to understand user behaviour in term of subscription. To be more specific, the end goal of this project is to find out whether there is a magic action that strongly correlates with/leads to subscription, so that:

  • Product owner can optimize user experience to drive more of these actions (1)
  • Marketers can expand targeted audience for UA campaign (2)

The approach:

To address this, we mined BHi data since 1st Jan, 2020 until 30th June, 2020, with the total number of 13 million users and 9,5k subscribers. We only investigate user behaviours on day0 as 50% subscriber comes from the first day (data from subscription team), and those events prior to "user_subscription". There are currently 949 events (both fired manually by tech-team and auto by Firebase) (see Appendix).

In this research, firstly we look into the impact of event existence on subscription: that is to see whether subscription rate change with or without those events. Then we look into if event frequency affects subs rate, and if do, at which frequency that those events will make the biggest impact.

We do not use normal Pearson correlation test, as the subscriber rate is so small (~0.07%) that can lead to bias result. Instead we use Information gain model, which enales us to tell how much more information that we could gain if a user take a specific action. For example, if user A experience event X, their chance of subscribing will be Y times higher (see Uplift in section Metrics collected). For more information, have a look at information gain & entropy: https://medium.com/coinmonks/what-is-entropy-and-why-information-gain-is-matter-4e85d46d2f01

The result:

Our contributions are as follows:

  • Not to mention the biggest uplift (see Metrics collected) of the event "in_app_purchase" of 281 times, "Subscription_Trial" of 60 times, "sub_offer_click" contributes the highest uplift in subscription rate (12 times)
  • When digging deeper into frequency, VideoAds_Show_NotReady has the highest uplift when it happens only once on the 1st day (12 times). It then drops dramatically on 2nd time of the day (to 1.1 times)

Metrics collected¶

We separate user into 2 groups (just like A/B testing): users/subscriber that experience a specific event and users/subscribers that DO NOT.

In order to access relation between subscription and events, these are metrics used:

  • num_user_true: number of user who have experienced the stated event
  • num_user_false: number of user who HAVE NOT experienced the stated event
  • num_subscriber_true: number of subscriber have experienced the stated event
  • num_subscriber_false: number of subscriber who HAVE NOT experienced the stated event
  • p_sub_true: subscription rate of those who have experienced the stated event (p_sub_true = num_subscriber_true/num_user_true)
  • p_sub_false: subscription rate of those who HAVE NOT experienced the stated event (p_sub_false = num_subscriber_false/num_user_false)
  • uplift: Difference in subscription rate between who have and do not have the stated event (uplift = p_sub_true/p_sub_false)
  • event_impact: reflect how much an event should be paid attention for its business impact (event_impact = uplift * subscriber_contributed) (will be explained in later section)

Research questions¶

Q1: Whether or not the existence of an event in user journey will affect on their chance of subscription?

Q2: Whether or not an event's frequency affect on their subscription chance uplift?

Q3: If we can find those magic actions, which are the ones that are scale-efficient to drive our user to experience those events?

Q2: Whether or not an event's frequency affect on their subscription chance uplift?¶

After getting the sense of which events have big impact on subscription rate, we then test to see if frequency of an event will affect its impact; and if have, at which that those events impact peak.

Event_number here is taken if that is the latest events happening before event "user_subscription". For example, if there is 5 event "user_engagement" happening before the user actually susbcribe, the only event is taken into consideration is 5th "user_engagement"

As can be seen in the chart below, trends are the later events that user encounter, the higher subscription chance they have. This is predictable as the more engagement of our game shows a higher subscription chance.

Event names that highly positively affect subscription probability to name are: in_app_purchase-16th event (really distinct impact), sub_offer_click...

Q3: If we can find those magic actions, which are the ones that are scale-efficient to drive our user to experience those events?¶

By now, we can already list some events that positively impact subscription uplift
However, to make sure the event has quite a "business impact", we also need to take number of user who has that event into consideration

As seen, those events that have big uplift in previous chart like 'in_app_purchase', 'Subscription_Trial' do not really account for big amount in total subscriber. In other words, most subscribers don't have these kinds of events for more than 2 times

Taking into consideration both uplift and subscriber_contributed is important. Therefore, we come up with the metric event_impact = uplift * subscriber_contributed to demonstrate the impact of that event on business as a whole

Conclusion: The event that has distinct uplift and big scale enough for MKT targeting is 'in_app_purchase' with the uplift of 189.

Suggestion for next steps: Continue to optimize based on those events related to subscription.

Limit¶

Within the scope of this research, the result just reflects correlation between events and subscription chance. To make sure they have causal impact, we suggest trying out an A/B testing in which suggests user when hitting the video_ads to subscribe instead to see whether there is causation in this case.
